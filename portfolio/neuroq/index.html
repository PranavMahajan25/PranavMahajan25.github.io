The story so far

This project lies at the two-way intersection of AI and Neuroscience, we wish to answer the following broad questions -

  • What is responsible for safer behaviours in animals and humans? What does pain neuroscience tell us? And how can we using it to induce safer behaviours in AI-agents?
  • How can we use concepts from AI and especially reinforcement learning to guide computational neuroscientific theories adressing anxiety, depression and pain?

A short summary of the things I’ve delved into, in the past half a year -

(1) Defining safety from a neuroscientific perspective - Safety in planning agents can mean a lot of things and previous formalization of safety includes a broad spectrum of definitions ranging from robot-like safety to animal like safety. Furthermore, in context of animal-like safety definitions, one can naively define it as pain accrued in an episode and cumulative pain accrued over episodes or say in terms of catastrophic deaths required to solve an environment. But better definitions also address risk (often treated as variance in returns) into what we call as “safe”, and better algorithms also include risk prediction to guide the policy!

(2) I initially explored a hypothesis - Is distributional RL safer than traditional RL?. We paused continuing in this direction, because C. Lyle et al. (2019) [1] showed using the coupled-updates method that distributional RL does not in fact produce lower variance value estimates and this suggests that it might just be more sample efficient than traditional RL and not necessarily safer unless of course we only use the lower tail of the distribution for the updates (for example CVaR, IQN [2]).

(3) In the meantime, there was a gap in the “safe deep RL” testing environments that needed to be addressed and thus developed Safety gym-minigrid which builds on gym-minigrid. (And will soon be released) Link to videos of the environment

(4) I am currently focusing on whether innate withdrawal actions and a pavlovian pain system makes agents safer, furthermore we would like to distinguish whether the algorithm is really “safe” or just more efficient.
