SyncBox

Quantifying synchronization in biological neural networks of our visual pathway.

We presented our initial work on a thalamocortical model in the Bernstein conference 2020 as a poster and are working towards a paper submission at the PAKDD conference, 2020. You can find the slides accompanying our poster presentation or watch a 5 minute summary video on Youtube!

Abstract

We present a study using a thalamocortical neural mass model to understand entrainment as an underlying mechanism of Steady State Visually Evoked Potentials observed in human Electroencephalogram (EEG) corresponding to periodically flickering stimuli [1]. Our model consists of the Lateral Geniculate Nucleus (LGN) [2], and the cortical Layers 4 and 6. The Relay cells (TCR) of the LGN project to the Pyramid cells of Layer 4 (Py4), while the TCR cells receive feedback from the Pyramid cells of Layer 6 (Py6) [3]. External stimuli to the model are simulated periodic signals with added white noise. The amplitude of the periodic components (k) is increased progressively, thus increasing the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) in the inputs. The dominant output frequency for all three populations of interest viz. TCR, Py4, and Py6 is 10 Hz when responding only to noise input, see Fig. 1(i). We plot the Phase Locking Value (PLV) for detuned input frequencies around a base input frequency of 10 Hz, see Fig. 1(ii); the PLV increases with increasing amplitude of the input stimulus. To understand the causal relations in the thalamocortical network model corresponding to the (noisy) periodic input, we have plotted the 1:1 phase locked region between the three above-mentioned cell populations, all of which display Arnold Tongue formation [1, 4], see Fig. 1(iv). This confirms our initial observation that starting at 10 Hz, the entrainment of the network is effective for a larger band of input frequencies with increasing input stimulus strength as demonstrated in [1]. Polar plots also show phase synchrony for high levels of SNR; the normalised Shannon Entropy (ρ) [1, 5] as an objective measure of phase synchrony validates the observations in the polar plots, see Fig. 1(v). The plots in Fig. 1(iii) show the development in the phase relations with time. For high entrainment, the phase locking remains constant over time and forms a plateau; for decreased phase locking, the slope of the plot increases indicating drifting apart of two phases, also termed as phase slips [1]. These plots help in understanding the phase relations in non-stationary signals. In summary, our work shows potential for the model to understand complex causal dynamics in the thalamocortical circuitry that underlie visual entrainment. Future work will build on and extend the work presented here to understand visual perception corresponding to flickering stimuli.

Figure 1: (i) Model base frequency is within the alpha band. The (ii) Phase Locking Value, (iii) phase difference, (v) polar histograms and normalised Shannon entropy (ρ) with increasing periodic input amplitude (k). (iv) Arnold tongue formations indicating 1:1 phase locked regions in the model.
