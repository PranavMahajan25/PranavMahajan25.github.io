ADReSS

Early detection of Alzheimer’s dementia through spontaneous speech

@ CNRL | Status : Active

Team members: Pranav Mahajan.

Supervisor: Dr. Veeky Baths.

This project started as a single author participation to the ADReSS Challenge shared-task at Interspeech 2020, which got weak rejected. After improving the work, under the guidance of Dr. Veeky, the paper is now under review at Frontiers research topic - ADReSS.

The research side of things

Current methods for early diagnosis of Alzheimer’s Dementia include structured questionnaires, structured interviews, and various cognitive tests. Language difficulties are a major problem in dementia as linguistic skills break down. Current methods do not provide robust tools to capture the true nature of language deficits in spontaneous speech. Early detection of Alzheimer’s Dementia from spontaneous speech overcomes the limitations of earlier approaches as it is less time consuming, can be done at home, and is relatively inexpensive. In this work, we re-implement the existing NLP methods, which used CNN-LSTM architectures and targeted features from conversational transcripts. Further, we build upon their work by devising an end-to-end deep learning-based solution that performs a binary classification of Alzheimer’s Dementia and predicts the MMSE score from the spontaneous speech of the patients. We utilize the ADReSS dataset for all our implementations and explore the deep learning-based methods of combining acoustic features into a common vector using recurrent units. We propose a bi-modal approach for both the tasks and discuss the merits and challenges of our approach.

Our attempt to include an acoustic feature processing stream (Speech-GRU) aims to address the following questions:

  • How to capture complex patterns and temporal relations in speech and language modalities?
  • And more importantly are there temporal patterns in the acoustic features extracted using the paralinguistic feature sets, which can prove to be useful early detection of AD?

The product development side of things

I am currently also leading a team of motivated software developers at BITS Goa, to develop applications which serve a dual purpose of collecting more AD speech data with ease as well as run inference to predict AD and it’s severity from spontaneous speech. We are focusing on extending to collecting AD and control speech data from low-resource languages in India, and our setting up collaborations with government hospitals and medical colleges (for example, B. J. medical college in Pune, Maharashtra). Languages like Hindi and Marathi are not exactly low-resource for other NLP tasks now with abundance of natural language data, but in context of AD speech and the cookie-theft picture paradigm, adds to the difficulty making it a low-resource spoken language problem. A primary motivation factor for this is that the models trained on the Dementia Bank dataset, even giving state-of-the-art performance on these datasets only cater to a small fraction of native english-speaking population of the 50 million people affected by AD each year.

Our initial outline can be found in this presentation. This work is supported by Technology Incubation Program at Center of Technical Education at BITS Goa.
